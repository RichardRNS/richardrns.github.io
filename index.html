Introduction to Spacecrafts

Introduction

Introduction to Spacecrafts

The launch of a spacecraft comprises a period of powered flight during which the vehicle rises above Earth's atmosphere and accelerates at least to orbital velocity. Powered flight ends when the rocket's last stage burns out, and the spacecraft separates and continues in freefall. If the spacecraft has achieved escape from Earth's gravitation, rather than entering Earth orbit, its flight path will then be purely a solar orbit of some description (NASA Science).




Spacecraft require an onboard source of electrical power to operate the equipment that they carry. Those designed to remain in Earth orbit for extended periods generally use panels of solar cells, often in conjunction with storage batteries. The shuttle orbiter, designed for stays in space of one to two weeks, uses hydrogen-oxygen fuel cells. Deep-space probes, such as the Galileo spacecraft that went into orbit around Jupiter in 1995 and the Cassini spacecraft launched to Saturn in 1997, are usually powered by small, long-lived radioisotope thermoelectric generators, which convert heat emitted by a radioactive element such as plutonium directly into electricity. (Britannica)




The purpose of a spacecraft mainly include:

  • Communication
  • Earth Observation
  • Meteorology
  • Navigation
  • Space/Planetary Colonization/Exploration
  • Transportation of Humans and Cargo




Actual vehicles are designed with a variety of shapes depending on the mission. Spacecraft differ greatly in size, shape, complexity, and purpose. Those that share similarities in design, function, or both are often grouped into program families. (Britannica)

Size

How big are these things?



A spacecraft must be able to fit inside a rocket fairing and the rocket must have enough power to lift it into orbit (ESA).
However, there is no strict rule of how big a spacecraft has to be. It could be as small as 12.25 cubic centimeters, or as big as 915.6 cubic meters.




International Space Station.
It has a size of 915 cubic meters and serves three main purposes: enable long-term space exploration, experimentation, and the gateway to space.

Mass

How heavy are these things?


The lightness of weight and functional reliability are primary features of spacecraft design (Britannica). This is because heavy spacecrafts need more fuels to launch, and fuels also have weight, thus needing more fuels to support those prior fuels, and ending in a positive feedback loop. However, as technology improve, this limitation is lifted as it is easier to find an equilibrium. For example, the first satellite ever launched was only weighed 83.6kg because of such limitations, but right now, we can easily launch communication satellites weighing more than 6 tonnes, or 6000kg. Some cargo carrier can weigh even more. For example, ESA's ATV weigh 20 tonnes. This allows more efficiency in performing tasks. For example, the ATV mentioned earlier can carry up to 9 tonnes of cargo and experiments to ISS in one flight.




International Space Station
The ISS have a mass of 419,700 kilograms, making it the heaviest spacecraft ever launched. This number would fluctuate as the amount of passenger and equipment on board change.




Kalamsat-V2
In 2019, a group of students in India launched the lightest spacecraft in history, Kalamsat-V2. It only weighs 1.26kg.
Surprisingly, the aforementioend "Sprites" is not the lightest. This is because although small, the design is extremely compact, giving Sprites a large mass.

Shape

What shape do they take?

When the Soviet Union and the United States flew the first real spaceships just nine years later—far sooner than most experts had predicted in 1952—they were anything but sleek. One was shaped like a bowling ball; the other resembled a Styrofoam coffee cup. (Air & Space Magazine) The shape of spacecraft then changed due to requirements in the cold war of ICBMs and supersonic travel, to depict a streamlined shape. However, a streamlined shape has no particular advantage in the vacuum and is harder to manufacture, so such shape was disposed after the cold war ended.




Since there is no air in space, a spacecraft can be more or less any shape. However, it is still partially dictated by whether they spin or not. Some satellites, such as Meteosat, spin and are drum-shaped. Most satellites today are box-shaped and do not spin. Landers is different. They have to withstand extreme heat as they descend through the air at high speed. For example, ESA’s Huygens probe, which landed on Titan, was shaped like a saucer, with a forward-facing heat shield. (European Space Agency)




For long stays in space, it is beneficial to have some sort of artificial gravity induced by rotation. Cylinders and toruses are then good options. They are also rather easy to inflate. Furthermore, spheres and cylinders are relatively easier to manufacture, hence making them popular in spacecraft designs. (Wired)

History

Brief history of spacecrafts

Brief history of space exploration

  1. Sputnik 1 - Oct.4, 1957 (First successful spacecraft launch)
  2. Laika - Nov. 3, 1957 (Second successful spacecraft launch, first creature in space)
  3. Able & Baker - May 28, 1959 (First creatures to return to Earth)
  4. Yuri Gagarin - April 12, 1961 (First human in space), Image provided on the right.
  5. Alexei Leonov - March 25, 1965 (First Spacewalk)
  6. Mariner 4 - June 14, 1965 (First picture of Mars)
  7. Luna - Feb. 3, 1966 (First spacecraft on moon)
  8. Venera 3 - March 1, 1966 (First spacecraft on Venus)
  9. Zond 5 - Sept. 15, 1968 (First spacecraft to orbit moon)
  10. Apollo 8 - Dec. 28, 1968 (First human on moon)
  11. Neil Armstrong - July 20, 1969 (First human to walk on moon)
  12. Salyut 1 - April 18, 1971 (First space station)
  13. Mars 3 - May 28, 1972 (First landing on Mars)
  14. Skylab I - May 14, 1973 (First orbiting labortory)
  15. Internatioanl Space Station - Nov.20 1998 (Biggest artificial body in space)

All above are completed with spacecrafts.




For an object to be recognized as a spacecraft, it needs to reach the altitude of 100km, or the Karman Line. This is also the line that is commonly recongized as the boundary between Earth and Space. Therefore, theoretically, there were many objects that should qualify as spacecrafts before Sputnik 1.
